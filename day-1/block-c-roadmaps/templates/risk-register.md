## Risk Response Strategies

_For each risk in your register, you should choose one of the four standard response strategies. Here is when to use each._

### Accept
**When to use:** The risk is low-likelihood and low-impact, or the cost of mitigation exceeds the expected cost of the risk materializing.
**Example:** "A competitor launches a similar analytics feature. We accept this risk because our differentiation is in execution speed, not feature uniqueness."
**Action:** Document the risk, monitor for trigger indicators, but do not invest resources in prevention.

### Mitigate
**When to use:** The risk is significant enough to warrant proactive investment, and there are concrete steps you can take to reduce likelihood or impact.
**Example:** "Key data engineer leaves. We mitigate by cross-training a second team member, documenting all pipelines, and maintaining a warm candidate pipeline for the role."
**Action:** Implement specific preventive measures. Assign an owner and a timeline.

### Transfer
**When to use:** The risk is better managed by another team, a vendor, or an insurance/contractual mechanism. Common for technical infrastructure and regulatory risks.
**Example:** "Cloud infrastructure outage causes data pipeline failure. We transfer this risk to our cloud provider via an SLA with financial penalties, and to our vendor via a support contract with guaranteed response times."
**Action:** Ensure contracts, SLAs, or agreements are in place. Verify the receiving party is aware and capable.

### Avoid
**When to use:** The risk is high-impact and can be eliminated entirely by changing the plan. This means giving something up — a feature, a timeline, or an approach.
**Example:** "Building a real-time ML scoring pipeline introduces reliability risks we cannot manage with current team size. We avoid this risk by using batch scoring with a 4-hour refresh, which meets 90% of the business need."
**Action:** Modify the roadmap to eliminate the risk-producing activity. Document what was traded off and why.

| Criterion | Excellent | Good | Needs Work |

| **Completeness** | 6+ risks spanning multiple categories; no obvious category is missing | 4-5 risks identified; some category gaps | Fewer than 4 risks or all risks are in the same category |
| **Specificity** | Risks are described precisely enough that someone else could monitor for them; trigger indicators are observable | Risks are identifiable but somewhat vague; trigger indicators present but generic | Risks are too abstract to act on ("things might go wrong") |
| **Top 3 Deep Dive** | Worst case, triggers, and mitigation are detailed and realistic; demonstrates genuine strategic thinking | Deep dive is present but feels formulaic; mitigations are generic | Deep dive is missing or superficial |
| **Response Strategy Fit** | Each risk has an appropriate response strategy with clear rationale for why that strategy was chosen | Response strategies assigned but rationale is thin | Response strategies missing or mismatched (e.g., "accepting" a high-likelihood, high-impact risk without justification) |
| **Integration with Roadmap** | Risks clearly connect to specific roadmap initiatives; risk register and roadmap tell a coherent story | Some connection to roadmap but not systematic | Risk register feels disconnected from the roadmap |
