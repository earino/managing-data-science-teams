## Context

_Where we are right now. Set the stage for your reader — someone two levels above you who has 3 minutes to read this. Cover: team composition, current capabilities, business environment, and key constraints. Write in complete sentences. No bullet points._

**Example (DataPulse context):**

> The analytics function at DataPulse is three months old. I am the first and only data hire, reporting directly to the CEO. The company has 50,000 monthly active users and is growing at approximately 15% month-over-month, with a premium conversion rate of 3.2%. We are 3-4 months from initiating a Series A fundraise, and our ability to present credible retention, LTV, and engagement metrics to investors is the primary business constraint driving our work. When I joined, there was no data warehouse, no agreed-upon metric definitions, and no reliable way to answer the question "how many active users do we actually have?" We have since stood up a lightweight data stack and established our first trusted metrics, but significant foundational work remains.

## What We've Done

_Key accomplishments and metrics moved. Be specific. Quantify everything. Use the BLUF principle: lead with the result, then explain how you got there._

**Guidance:**
- What did the team ship or deliver in the most recent period?
- What metrics moved as a result? By how much?
- What was the business impact in terms the reader cares about (revenue, cost, risk, speed)?
- If you are writing this at the beginning of your tenure (as in the case contexts), describe what you have accomplished in your first 30-90 days.

_Use plausible fictional metrics for your case context. The skill being assessed is the structure and clarity of the writing, not the accuracy of the invented numbers._

## What We've Learned

_Insights, surprises, and pivots. This section demonstrates intellectual honesty and analytical maturity. Executives value leaders who learn from data, not just report it._

**Guidance:**
- What surprised you? What did you expect to find that turned out differently?
- What did you learn about the business, the data, or your team that changes how you think about the next quarter?
- Were there any pivots — things you planned to do but stopped because the data told you otherwise?
- This section is where you build trust. Showing what you got wrong is more credible than pretending everything went perfectly.

[Your What We've Learned section here — aim for one substantial paragraph, approximately 80-120 words.]

## What's Next

_Priorities for the next quarter, with rationale. This should connect directly to your roadmap's Now column. For each priority, explain WHY it matters to the business, not just what it is._

**Guidance:**
- List your top 3 priorities for the next quarter.
- For each, explain: what it is, why it matters to the business (not to your team), and what success looks like.
- This section should make the reader feel confident that you have a clear plan and sound judgment about where to invest your team's time.
- Reference your North Star metric — how do these priorities move it?

[Your What's Next section here — aim for one substantial paragraph per priority, approximately 150-200 words total.]

## What We Need

_Asks and blockers. Be direct. Executives respect clarity about what you need to succeed. Vague asks ("more resources") get ignored. Specific asks ("one senior analytics engineer by Q2, approved through the standard headcount process") get funded._

**Guidance:**
- What do you need from leadership? (headcount, budget, executive sponsorship, decisions)
- What do you need from peer teams? (engineering support, product alignment, legal review)
- What blockers exist that you cannot resolve yourself?
- For each ask, explain the consequence of not getting it: "Without X, we will be unable to deliver Y, which puts Z at risk."

[Your What We Need section here — aim for 3-5 specific, actionable asks with rationale, approximately 100-150 words.]

## Writing Checklist

Before submitting, verify:

- [ ] Written entirely in prose (no bullet-point lists in the body)
- [ ] Leads with the most important information in each section (BLUF)
- [ ] No jargon — a non-technical executive can understand every sentence
- [ ] All claims are quantified (dollars, percentages, user counts, time saved)
- [ ] Fits within 2 pages when formatted (approximately 800-1,000 words total)
- [ ] Each section flows logically into the next
- [ ] The "What We Need" section has specific, actionable asks — not vague requests

---

## Evaluation Criteria

This artifact will be assessed as part of the Roadmap + Exec Narrative portfolio component (25% of course grade). For the Day 1 Checkpoint, only the Context and What's Next sections are required (as drafts).

**For the final portfolio submission, this narrative will be evaluated on:**

| Criterion | Excellent | Good | Needs Work |
|-----------|-----------|------|------------|
| **Clarity & Structure** | Five sections flow logically; each section has a clear purpose; the reader never wonders "why am I reading this?" | Structure is sound but transitions are abrupt or one section is significantly weaker | Sections feel disconnected or the narrative reads like a status update rather than a strategic document |
| **BLUF & Executive Voice** | Every section leads with the key insight; language is direct, jargon-free, and quantified | Mostly leads with answers but occasionally buries the key point; some jargon slips through | Key conclusions are buried; reads like a technical report, not an executive communication |
| **Business Alignment** | Every priority and ask is connected to a business outcome the reader cares about | Most items have business rationale but some feel internally focused | Reads like a team update rather than a business strategy document |
| **Intellectual Honesty** | "What We Learned" section shows genuine reflection; acknowledges surprises and course corrections | Some reflection present but feels surface-level | "What We Learned" is missing or is just disguised good news |
| **Specificity of Asks** | "What We Need" has 3+ specific, actionable requests with clear consequences if not met | Asks are present but vague ("more resources") or missing consequence framing | No asks, or asks are so vague they cannot be acted upon |
