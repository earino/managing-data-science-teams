## Scoring Dimensions

*Define 4–6 dimensions that capture what matters for this role. Each dimension should map to a 90-day outcome or key responsibility from your job description. Use the 1–4 scale below — no fence-sitting on a 3-point scale.*

### Dimension Definitions and Behavioral Anchors

| Dimension | 1 — Novice | 2 — Developing | 3 — Proficient | 4 — Expert |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Technical Execution** | Analysis contains significant errors; inappropriate methods; does not handle data quality issues | Analysis is mostly correct but surface-level; misses some edge cases; limited tool proficiency | Sound methodology; handles edge cases; clean, reproducible work; appropriate tool selection | Sophisticated approach; validates assumptions; considers alternative methods; exceptional craft |
| **Communication Clarity** | Findings are unclear; heavy jargon; no narrative structure; audience cannot act on the output | States findings but does not connect to business context; some jargon; structure is loose | Clear narrative; connects analysis to decisions; appropriate for the audience; well-organized | Compelling and concise; a non-technical executive could act on it immediately; anticipates questions |
| **Business Context Awareness** | Does not connect analysis to business outcomes; treats the problem as purely technical | Acknowledges business context but does not use it to shape the analysis or recommendation | Frames analysis in terms of business impact; recommendation reflects understanding of constraints | Reframes the question to address what the stakeholder actually needs; identifies second-order effects |
| **Intellectual Curiosity** | Answers only what is asked; no follow-up questions; does not explore beyond the surface | Shows some curiosity but does not pursue it systematically | Asks good questions; explores beyond the obvious; identifies gaps in the data or analysis | Generates new questions the team had not considered; proposes next steps that advance understanding |
| **Collaboration Signal** | Dismissive of others' input; does not seek feedback; works in isolation | Receptive to feedback but does not actively seek input; limited stakeholder awareness | Actively seeks input; demonstrates experience working across functions; values diverse perspectives | Builds on others' ideas; creates shared understanding; has a track record of cross-functional impact |

**Guidance on scoring:**
- **1 (Novice):** Below the bar for this level. Clear no-hire signal on this dimension.
- **2 (Developing):** Approaching the bar but not yet there. Acceptable only if other dimensions are strong.
- **3 (Proficient):** Meets expectations for this level. This is the hiring bar.
- **4 (Expert):** Exceeds expectations. Rare — reserve for genuinely exceptional performance.

## Red Flags & Green Flags

*Pre-define what signals should raise concern or increase confidence. This prevents post-hoc rationalization.*

### Red Flags (Investigate Further or Proceed with Caution)

| Signal | Why It Matters | How to Probe |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Cannot explain methodology in plain language | Analytics requires communicating to non-technical stakeholders | Ask: "How would you explain this to a product manager?" |
| Blames previous employers or colleagues | Pattern of external attribution suggests low ownership | Ask: "Tell me about a project that didn't go well. What was your role in that?" |
| No questions about the business or team | May indicate low curiosity or purely transactional motivation | Note whether candidate asks substantive questions during the interview |
| Oversells AI/ML without grounding | May prioritize impressive techniques over business impact | Ask: "Walk me through when you chose a simple approach over a complex one" |
| Dismissive of data quality concerns | Will produce unreliable analyses | Ask: "What is the messiest dataset you have worked with? How did you handle it?" |

### Green Flags (Positive Signals to Seek Out)

| Signal | Why It Matters | How to Probe |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Asks clarifying questions before diving in | Indicates strong problem framing and stakeholder awareness | Present an ambiguous problem and observe whether they ask questions or assume |
| Acknowledges what they don't know | Intellectual honesty prevents costly analytical errors | Ask: "What is something in this domain you would need to learn?" |
| References impact on decisions, not just analyses | Outcome orientation over activity orientation | Ask: "Tell me about an analysis that changed a decision" |
| Proactively identifies tradeoffs | Shows mature judgment and systems thinking | Present a resource-constrained scenario and observe how they prioritize |
| Gives credit to teammates | Collaboration orientation and low ego | Ask about a team accomplishment and listen for "we" vs. "I" |

## Evaluation Criteria

This rubric will be assessed on the following dimensions:

| Criterion | What We Are Looking For |
|:---|:---|
| **Dimension Selection** | Are the scoring dimensions relevant to the role? Do they map to 90-day outcomes? Are there 4–6 dimensions (not too few, not too many)? |
| **Behavioral Anchors** | Are the 1–4 descriptions specific enough that two independent scorers would assign similar scores? |
| **Calibration Readiness** | Could a new interviewer use this rubric with minimal training? Is the norming section filled in thoughtfully? |
| **Red/Green Flags** | Are the flags specific, observable, and tied to job-relevant signals (not personal preferences)? |
| **Completeness** | Is the scorecard filled in with realistic content? Is the calibration section addressed? |
