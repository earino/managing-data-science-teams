## Pre-Class Setup

### Room Arrangement
- **Table groups of 4–5 students.** If the room has fixed rows, ask students to cluster into groups at the start.
- Each group should ideally have a mix of case contexts (Small / Medium / Large). If students have already chosen contexts before class, try to distribute them. If not, have them choose in the first 5 minutes.
- Ensure every group can see the projector/screen and has enough table space for laptops or printed templates.

### Materials Needed
- **Projected slides** — Marp deck rendered to PDF or presented via Marp CLI
- **Templates** — Either printed copies of all four templates (team-charter, stakeholder-map, raci, decision-memo) OR ensure students have laptop access to the digital versions. Recommend digital for editing convenience.
- **Whiteboard or flip chart** — Useful for the debrief; you may want to sketch the Power/Interest grid live.
- **Timer visible to students** — Use a phone timer projected or a large classroom clock. Students work better with visible countdowns.
- **Case context cards** — If not already distributed, have the three case descriptions (Small/Medium/Large from the syllabus) available as a handout or on-screen.

### What to Have on Screen at 11:00
- Title slide displayed as students enter
- LMS or course page open in a tab for reference
- Timer app ready

## Key Teaching Points

### Must Emphasize
1. **Grove's output equation is the foundation.** Keep returning to it throughout the day. The manager's output is not their own work — it's the output of their team and influenced teams. This reframe from "boss" to "system builder" should be the throughline.
2. **The Manager OS is a system, not a checklist.** The four layers (cadences, rituals, artifacts, decision hygiene) interact. A cadence without artifacts is just meetings. Artifacts without cadences are just documents nobody reads.
3. **Type 1 vs. Type 2 decisions.** This framework from Bezos is the single most useful decision-making heuristic for new managers. Most decisions are Type 2 (reversible). Default to speed on those.
4. **1:1s are sacred.** The anti-patterns slide usually generates strong reactions. Let students share their own bad 1:1 experiences — this builds rapport and drives the point home.
5. **Specificity in charters matters.** Push students away from generic statements. "We value quality" means nothing. "Every dashboard has a documented owner and a review date" is actionable.

### Skip If Short on Time
- **RACI slide (slide 11)** — Can be covered in 60 seconds or skipped entirely; the template is self-explanatory. Students can fill in the RACI template as homework.
- **Team Topologies details (slide 4)** — If pressed, mention the framework and tell students to read the book. The key takeaway is just "there are different types of analytics teams."
- **Pair Share can be shortened to 6 minutes total (3 min each)** if running behind.

### Do Not Skip
- The Team Charter activity (25 min) — This is the core deliverable of the block.
- The Stakeholder Map activity (20 min) — Also a core deliverable.
- At least a brief debrief — Even 2 minutes of whole-room discussion cements the learning.

## Common Student Questions and Suggested Responses

### 1. "I've never been a manager. How do I know what stakeholders to map?"
**Response:** "That's exactly why we're doing this exercise. Think about it from first principles. Your team produces analytics outputs — who consumes them? Your team needs data — who controls the data sources? Your team has a budget — who approves it? Follow the dependency chains and you'll find your stakeholders. Also, look at your case context description — it names several teams and constraints. Start there."

### 2. "What if my case context doesn't specify enough detail for a charter?"
**Response:** "Make reasonable assumptions and state them. In the real world, you'll never have perfect information when writing a charter either. Write what you know, flag what you're assuming, and note what you'd want to validate. That's actually a great meta-skill for a manager — being explicit about your assumptions."

### 3. "How is the Team Charter different from a team mission statement?"
**Response:** "A mission statement is one element of the charter — the Purpose section. The charter goes further: it defines scope (what you don't do is as important as what you do), principles (how you operate day-to-day), interfaces (who you work with), and success metrics (how you know if you're winning). Think of the mission as the 'why' and the charter as the full 'how we operate' document."

### 4. "In a small startup, do you really need all these cadences and artifacts?"
**Response:** "Great question. No — you scale the OS to the context. A 3-person startup team doesn't need a quarterly calibration process. But they absolutely need a weekly sync, a shared understanding of scope, and a way to make and record decisions. The charter might be half a page. The stakeholder map might have 4 people on it. That's fine. The framework scales; the formality doesn't have to."

### 5. "Is the Team Charter a living document or a one-time thing?"
**Response:** "Living, absolutely. Review it quarterly at minimum. The charter you write today is a draft — you'll refine it as you learn more throughout the course and especially after the hiring block. Real charters evolve as the team grows, the business changes, and you learn what works. The point is to have an explicit, written agreement rather than implicit assumptions that nobody ever validated."

### 6. "How do I handle a stakeholder who is high power but actively hostile to analytics?"
**Response:** "That's a 'Keep Satisfied' stakeholder and potentially the most important one on your map. You can't ignore them. Your strategy is usually: find out what they care about, demonstrate value in their terms, and build the relationship before you need something from them. We'll talk more about 'leading up and across' on Day 2, Block F. For now, just map them honestly."

### 7. "Should I use OKRs or KPIs for the success metrics in my charter?"
**Response:** "Either framework works. The key is that your metrics are specific, measurable, and tied to business outcomes — not vanity metrics. 'Increase dashboard usage' is weak. 'Reduce time-to-insight for the Growth PM from 3 days to same-day for standard queries' is strong because it's outcome-oriented. Pick whichever framework your case context would realistically use."

## Energy Management

This is the **opening block** of a two-day intensive. Energy management is critical.

### Setting the Tone (First 10 Minutes)
- **Start on time.** This signals professionalism and respect for students' time. It also models what you'll teach about cadences.
- **Be warm but direct.** The Grove quote sets a slightly provocative, intellectual tone. Lean into it.
- **Ask the room a question early** (within the first 5 minutes): "Who here has had a manager? Who here has been a manager? Who here never wants to be a manager?" This gets people talking and lowers the barrier to participation.
- **Name the format:** "This is a workshop, not a lecture. I'll teach concepts in short bursts, then you'll build real artifacts. You'll leave today with work you can actually use."

### Building Psychological Safety
- **Normalize not knowing.** These are MSBA students, not seasoned managers. Say explicitly: "You are not expected to know this. The point of the exercise is to practice thinking like a manager."
- **During activities, circulate actively.** Visit every table. Ask "what are you stuck on?" rather than "how's it going?" The former gives permission to struggle.
- **In the debrief, ask for volunteers — don't cold-call.** This is the first block; trust hasn't been built yet. By Day 2, you can push harder.
- **Praise specificity.** When a student shares a charter principle or a stakeholder insight, say "That's specific and actionable — that's exactly what a good charter looks like."

### Energy Through the Block
- **11:00–11:30 (lecture portion):** Keep pace brisk. These slides are concept-heavy. If energy dips, ask a question: "Quick show of hands — who has been in a meeting that should have been a memo?"
- **11:30–11:45 (final concepts + activity brief):** Students may check out briefly. The activity brief re-engages them. Be crisp: "Here's what you're doing, here's how long you have, go."
- **11:45–12:30 (activities):** This is the high-energy portion. Circulate, coach, encourage. Students working on real artifacts stay engaged.
- **12:30–12:40 (pair share + debrief):** Energy often dips at the end. Keep the debrief tight and end with a forward-looking question: "What role would you hire first?" This primes them for Block B.

## Contingencies

### If Running 10 Minutes Behind
- **Cut the RACI slide** (slide 11) — students can learn RACI from the template. Save 3 minutes.
- **Shorten the pair share** from 10 minutes to 5 minutes (2.5 min each). Save 5 minutes.
- **Shorten the debrief** from 3 minutes to 1 minute — just ask one question and give the recap. Save 2 minutes.
- Do NOT cut the Team Charter or Stakeholder Map activities. These are the core deliverables.

### If Running 10 Minutes Ahead
- **Expand the pair share** to 15 minutes. Have pairs also share their Team Charter, not just the stakeholder map.
- **Deeper debrief** — ask 2-3 volunteers to share instead of 1. Facilitate a brief discussion about how different case contexts produced different charters.
- **Introduce the decision memo template** — walk through a quick example on screen. This primes students for later blocks where they'll use it.

### If Technology Fails (No Projector)
- The slides are primarily discussion prompts. You can teach this block from the facilitator notes alone.
- Write the Grove quote on the whiteboard. Draw the Power/Interest grid. Talk through the concepts.
- Ensure students have template access on their laptops (digital) or as printouts.

### If a Student Dominates Discussion
- Thank them for their contribution and redirect: "Great point. Let's hear from someone who hasn't spoken yet."
- During pair share, you can strategically pair a dominant student with a more confident peer.

### If Students Finish Activities Early
- Ask them to review their charter against the evaluation criteria on the template.
- Suggest they start the RACI template or the decision memo template as a stretch activity.
- Encourage them to help neighboring students who might be stuck.

## Transition Notes: Bridging to Block B

Block B (13:30–15:10) covers **Hiring & Team Formation**. The bridge should feel natural:

**Key narrative link:** "You've defined what your team does (charter) and who your team interacts with (stakeholder map). The next question is: who should be ON your team?"

**Lunch prompt:** Give students something to think about over lunch: "Look at your charter. What's the first role you'd hire to fulfill that charter? Come back ready to answer that."

**What Block B assumes students already have:**
- A case context chosen and understood
- A draft Team Charter (purpose, scope, principles)
- A draft Stakeholder Map (at least 6 stakeholders mapped)
- Familiarity with the Manager OS framework (cadences, rituals, artifacts, decision hygiene)

**If Block A ran short and charters/maps are thin:** Allocate the first 10 minutes of Block B for students to finish up. Adjust Block B timing accordingly.
