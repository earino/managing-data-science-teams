# FinGuard: Modernizing Analytics at a 120-Year-Old Regional Bank

## Company Profile

FinGuard National Bank is a publicly traded regional bank headquartered in the American Midwest, with $18 billion in assets under management, 5,000 employees, and 142 branch locations across six states. The bank was founded in 1906, survived two world wars, the Great Depression, and the 2008 financial crisis. It has been publicly traded since 1971.

FinGuard operates three major business lines: Retail Banking (consumer checking, savings, mortgages, and personal lending), Commercial Lending (small and mid-market business loans, commercial real estate), and Wealth Management (private banking and advisory services for high-net-worth clients). Each business line operates with significant autonomy, its own P&L, and its own ideas about what "analytics" means.

Two years ago, the board of directors approved a $5M multi-year investment in enterprise analytics as part of a broader digital transformation initiative. The stated goal was to "become a data-driven organization that uses advanced analytics to improve customer experience, manage risk, and drive operational efficiency." The Chief Data Officer, who was hired to lead this transformation, made commitments to the board about ROI timelines that are now approaching. The board wants evidence that the $5M was well spent.

FinGuard operates under heavy regulatory oversight. The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) conducts regular examinations. The bank is subject to SOX compliance for financial reporting, GDPR for its small but growing European customer base (accessed through its wealth management division), and a constellation of banking-specific data regulations including BSA/AML requirements. A full regulatory examination is scheduled in six months, and the examiners have signaled interest in the bank's model governance practices and data management maturity.

## Current Data State

FinGuard's data infrastructure is a geological record of technology decisions spanning three decades. Each layer was state-of-the-art when it was built. Together, they form a complex, fragile, and politically charged ecosystem.

**The Teradata warehouse** has been the backbone of FinGuard's data infrastructure for fifteen years. It houses the bank's core reporting data — transaction histories, account information, customer demographics, loan performance data, and regulatory reporting tables. The warehouse is fed by Informatica ETL pipelines that extract data nightly from the bank's core banking system (FIS), the mortgage origination platform, and various operational databases. The Teradata license costs $800,000 per year, and the hardware is approaching end-of-life. Approximately 400 scheduled reports run against Teradata daily, serving business units, risk management, regulatory reporting, and finance.

**Informatica ETL** is maintained by two senior developers, both of whom have been at the bank for over 20 years and are approaching retirement within the next 2-3 years. They are the only people who fully understand the 600+ ETL jobs that move data into and around Teradata. Documentation is sparse — much of the institutional knowledge lives in their heads. They are cooperative but skeptical of change, and they have seen multiple "modernization" initiatives come and go without replacing their work.

**Tableau Server** hosts over 200 dashboards built over the past six years. An internal audit last quarter found that approximately 60% of these dashboards are either broken (due to upstream data changes), stale (not updated in 90+ days), or abandoned (no views in 6 months). The remaining 40% are actively used, but many contain hardcoded logic that duplicates or contradicts what is in Teradata. The Tableau license costs $400,000 per year and is up for renewal in 10 months.

**Databricks on AWS** is a three-month-old pilot project, approved after a lengthy procurement process. Five data scientists and engineers have access. They are using it for a customer churn prediction model and a transaction fraud detection prototype. The pilot is technically promising but politically controversial — the IT infrastructure team views it as a shadow IT initiative, and the compliance team has not yet fully reviewed the data governance implications of having banking data in a cloud environment. Only synthetic and anonymized data has been used so far; getting production data into Databricks requires clearing multiple security and compliance reviews.

**Shadow analytics teams** exist in each of the three business lines. Retail Banking has two business analysts who build Excel models and Access databases to track branch performance, campaign effectiveness, and customer segmentation. Commercial Lending has one analyst who maintains a complex Excel workbook that models loan portfolio risk — a workbook so critical that the business line head has called it "the most important spreadsheet in the bank." Wealth Management has a junior analyst who pulls data from a third-party portfolio management system and manually reconciles it with internal data. These shadow teams produce numbers that frequently conflict with what the central analytics team reports, leading to contentious debates in quarterly business reviews.

**Data mesh aspirations** were introduced by the CDO six months ago as a long-term operating model. The idea is to shift toward domain-owned data products with centralized governance standards. A governance committee has been formed and meets monthly. Progress is slow — the committee has spent three meetings debating the definition of "data product" without reaching consensus. The business line heads view data mesh as a way to get more autonomy; central IT views it as a way to push accountability to the business; and the CDO views it as a way to scale analytics without growing the central team linearly. These conflicting interpretations have not yet been reconciled.

## Key Stakeholders

**David Okonkwo, Chief Data Officer.** David is your direct manager. He was hired three years ago from a large consulting firm to lead FinGuard's data transformation. He secured the $5M board investment on the promise of measurable ROI within three years — a deadline that is now 14 months away. David is politically savvy and genuinely committed to modernization, but he is under significant pressure. He needs your team to produce visible wins that he can present to the board while simultaneously building the long-term foundation. He will shield you from some organizational friction, but he cannot protect you from the board's impatience.

**Margaret Huang, Head of Risk and Compliance.** Margaret oversees regulatory reporting, model risk management, BSA/AML compliance, and the upcoming OCC examination. She is the most powerful stakeholder you cannot afford to alienate. Her primary concern is model governance: the data science team has deployed several ML models (churn prediction, fraud scoring, credit risk supplements) without a formal model validation framework, model inventory, or ongoing monitoring process. The OCC examiners will ask about this. Margaret needs a model governance framework in place before the examination, and she needs every model to meet explainability requirements. She is not opposed to innovation, but she will block anything that creates regulatory risk.

**Victor Stanhope, VP of IT Infrastructure.** Victor controls all technology procurement, infrastructure provisioning, and security reviews at FinGuard. His team manages the on-premises data center, network security, and the bank's nascent cloud presence. All new vendor relationships require a 90-day minimum onboarding process that includes security assessment, legal review, and architecture review. All data — especially customer data — must reside in approved environments that meet banking regulatory requirements. Victor is not obstructionist by nature, but his incentives are aligned with stability and security, not speed. He views the Databricks pilot with suspicion and the data mesh initiative as premature. He is a critical gatekeeper, and any infrastructure decision you make will require his sign-off.

**Business Line Heads: Karen Walsh (Retail Banking), Thomas Ingram (Commercial Lending), and Patricia Delgado (Wealth Management).** Each of these stakeholders controls a P&L and has their own analytics resources, however small. They are skeptical of centralized analytics because past attempts at centralization resulted in slower turnaround times and reports that did not reflect the nuances of their business. They will engage with your team if you can demonstrate that centralized capabilities make their analysts more effective rather than less autonomous. They are not going to give up their shadow analytics teams willingly — you need to give them a reason to want to.

**Richard Tse, CISO.** Richard is leading a zero-trust data access initiative that will affect how every team in the bank accesses data. The initiative is well-intentioned but is adding friction to data workflows across the organization. Richard's team is auditing all existing data access patterns and will be implementing role-based access controls that are significantly more restrictive than the current environment. Your team will need to work within these new constraints, and any data platform architecture must align with the zero-trust model.

## Pain Points

- **Procurement paralysis.** The 18-month typical procurement cycle for new enterprise tools (90-day vendor onboarding, security review, legal review, architecture review, budget approval, pilot, rollout) means that by the time a tool is approved, the original business need has often shifted. The Databricks pilot took 11 months from initial request to first user login. This pace is incompatible with the CDO's board commitments.
- **Conflicting numbers at the board level.** The most politically damaging problem is that the central analytics team, the business line shadow teams, and the finance department regularly present different numbers for the same metrics in board meetings and executive reviews. Customer count, revenue by segment, loan delinquency rates — each constituency has its own calculation, its own data source, and its own version of the truth. This undermines confidence in all data and in the analytics function itself.
- **Teradata migration minefield.** Everyone agrees that the Teradata warehouse needs to be modernized, but nobody agrees on what "modernize" means. The CDO wants to migrate to cloud (Databricks or Snowflake). IT wants to upgrade Teradata to a newer version. The Informatica developers want to keep the current architecture and just replace the hardware. The business lines do not want to be disrupted. The 400 daily reports that run on Teradata cannot go down, and the two people who understand the ETL pipelines are approaching retirement. This is not just a technical migration — it is a political negotiation.
- **Unmonitored ML models.** The data science team has deployed models into production without a formal governance framework. There is no model inventory, no performance monitoring, no drift detection, and no documented validation process. Margaret's risk team discovered this during a routine review and escalated it. The OCC examiners will almost certainly ask about model risk management, and the current state is indefensible.
- **Single points of failure.** The two Informatica developers represent the most acute people risk in the data organization. If either one were to leave unexpectedly, critical ETL pipelines that feed regulatory reports would be at risk. Knowledge transfer has been discussed repeatedly but never prioritized, partly because the developers themselves are reluctant to document processes they have been refining for two decades.
- **Shadow analytics trust deficit.** The business line analysts produce work that is deeply embedded in operational decision-making. The "most important spreadsheet in the bank" in Commercial Lending is a real and significant risk — a single Excel error could lead to mispriced loans or inaccurate risk reporting. But telling a business line head that their trusted analyst's work is a compliance risk is a conversation that requires extraordinary political skill.

## Constraints

- **Budget reality.** The $2M annual budget sounds substantial, but it is largely committed. The Teradata license consumes $800,000. Tableau costs $400,000. Informatica licensing and maintenance costs $200,000. That leaves approximately $600,000 in discretionary spending for the year — covering the Databricks pilot costs, any new tooling, training, consulting, and contingency. Any significant new investment requires either sunsetting an existing tool or requesting additional budget from the board, which means demonstrating ROI on the current investment first.
- **Team composition.** Your central team of 15 people includes a mix of legacy SQL report developers (5 people, deep Teradata expertise, limited modern tooling experience), data scientists (3 people, strong in Python/ML, limited production engineering skills), data engineers (2 people, supporting both legacy Informatica and the new Databricks pilot), reporting analysts (3 people, primarily Tableau and Excel), and two managers (one overseeing reporting, one overseeing data science). The skill distribution does not match the work that needs to be done. Retraining is possible but slow, and union considerations mean that role changes must be handled carefully through HR and with appropriate notice.
- **Security and compliance overhead.** Every new tool, every new data flow, and every new access pattern requires IT security review (4-6 weeks), compliance review (2-4 weeks), and often legal review (2-3 weeks). These reviews can run in parallel in some cases, but the effective minimum time from proposal to approval for anything non-trivial is 6-8 weeks. Cloud data usage requires additional documentation demonstrating compliance with banking regulations, including OCC guidance on cloud computing and third-party risk management.
- **Regulatory examination timeline.** The OCC examination in six months is a hard deadline. Model governance, data lineage documentation, and regulatory reporting accuracy must be demonstrably improved before the examiners arrive. Failing an examination has consequences that extend well beyond the analytics team — it affects the entire bank.
- **Organizational culture.** FinGuard has a conservative, risk-averse culture shaped by 120 years of banking. Change happens slowly and by consensus. The data mesh concept is appealing in theory but culturally foreign. Any modernization effort that is perceived as moving too fast, taking unnecessary risks, or disrespecting institutional knowledge will face passive resistance that is far more difficult to overcome than active opposition.

## Your Role

You are the VP of Enterprise Analytics at FinGuard National Bank, reporting to CDO David Okonkwo. You have been in the role for two years — long enough to understand the landscape, build some relationships, and accomplish a few early wins (consolidating the reporting team, launching the Databricks pilot, starting the data governance committee). But you are now at the inflection point where the hard problems can no longer be deferred.

You manage a team of 15 people with uneven skills and mixed enthusiasm for change. You have stakeholders who range from strong allies (the CDO) to cautious gatekeepers (IT, compliance) to skeptical customers (business line heads). You have a $2M budget that is 70% committed to legacy infrastructure. And you have a regulatory examination in six months that will test whether the analytics organization can meet the standards expected of a regulated financial institution.

Your challenge is to modernize a data organization in an environment where every change requires navigating politics, procurement, compliance, and culture. You must keep the existing reporting infrastructure running while building the next generation of analytics capabilities. You must establish model governance and data quality standards that satisfy regulators without paralyzing the data science team. You must find a path forward on the Teradata migration that is technically sound, financially viable, and politically acceptable. And you must do all of this while demonstrating the ROI that the CDO promised the board — because if that ROI does not materialize, the entire analytics investment may be reconsidered.

The most important skill you bring to this role is not technical. It is the ability to build coalitions, sequence initiatives so that early wins fund later ambitions, and communicate in terms that each stakeholder group — the board, the regulators, the engineers, the business line leaders — can understand and support.
