# Mock Calibration — Performance Profiles

Use these three profiles for the mock calibration exercise. Each group of 4 students independently rates all three profiles, then reaches consensus as a group.

**Rating Scale:** Exceeds Expectations / Meets Expectations / Below Expectations

**Important:** Rate each profile relative to their **current level**, not in the abstract. What "meets expectations" looks like is different for an L2 vs. an L4.

---

## Profile A: Dana — L3 Senior Analyst, 2.5 years at company

**Role Context:** Senior Analyst on the product analytics team. Expected at this level: independently scope and execute analyses, mentor junior analysts, deliver insights that influence product decisions.

### Key Accomplishments (Past 6 Months)

1. **Retention Deep-Dive:** Independently identified that 30-day retention had dropped 4 percentage points. Scoped a cohort analysis, discovered the drop was concentrated in users acquired through a new paid channel. Product team paused the channel, saving an estimated $200K in wasted acquisition spend. Analysis was cited in the board deck.

2. **Dashboard Consolidation:** Consolidated 12 one-off dashboards into 3 self-serve views, reducing ad hoc requests to the analytics team by ~40%. Stakeholders praised the clarity.

3. **Mentoring:** Onboarded a new L2 analyst. The L2 shipped their first independent analysis in week 4 (team average is week 8). Dana wrote a 5-page onboarding guide that's now used for all new hires.

### Development Areas

- **Stakeholder management:** Tends to deliver results via Slack messages rather than structured presentations. The VP of Product noted: "Dana's work is excellent but I sometimes miss it because it arrives as a wall of text in a thread."
- **Prioritization:** Took on the dashboard consolidation project without checking with her manager. It was valuable but delayed the A/B test analysis by two weeks.
- **Technical depth:** SQL is strong, but has avoided learning Python. Some analyses require statistical methods that are hard to implement in SQL alone.

### Manager's Summary Note
"Dana is one of our strongest analysts. Her retention work was genuinely impactful. My concern is that she operates as a brilliant individual contributor but doesn't yet amplify her impact through stakeholder relationships or systematic communication. She's also avoiding the Python gap, which will limit her at L4."

## Profile B: Kevin — L4 Staff Data Scientist, 4 years at company

**Role Context:** Staff Data Scientist on the experimentation platform team. Expected at this level: set technical direction for the team, drive cross-functional initiatives, deliver L5-scope impact, mentor L3s.

### Key Accomplishments (Past 6 Months)

1. **Experimentation Framework:** Led the design and rollout of a new Bayesian experimentation framework that replaced the previous frequentist approach. Reduced average experiment duration by 30%. Published an internal tech blog post explaining the methodology.

2. **Cross-Functional Partnership:** Worked with Engineering to instrument 15 new product events, filling critical gaps in the event taxonomy. This unblocked 3 product teams that had been waiting for tracking data.

3. **Conference Talk:** Presented the experimentation framework at a regional data science meetup. Positive reception; two candidates mentioned it in interviews as a reason they applied.

### Development Areas

- **Delivery consistency:** The experimentation framework launched 6 weeks late. Kevin attributed delays to scope changes from Engineering, but his manager noted that Kevin repeatedly expanded scope himself ("while we're at it, let's also add...").
- **Team dynamics:** Two L3 data scientists on the team have expressed frustration that Kevin "takes the interesting problems and delegates the boring ones." One described feeling "like Kevin's data cleaning crew."
- **Written communication:** Kevin's documentation is minimal. The experimentation framework has a 2-page README. The team has asked for proper runbooks multiple times.

### Manager's Summary Note
"Kevin is technically brilliant and his experimentation work is genuinely field-leading for a company our size. But I'm struggling with two things. First, the scope creep pattern — he's a victim of his own ambition and it affects timelines. Second, the team dynamics issue concerns me. An L4 should be elevating the people around them. Kevin is elevating the work but not the team. I'm torn on the rating."

## Profile C: Mika — L2 Analyst, 11 months at company

**Role Context:** Junior Analyst on the growth team. Expected at this level: execute well-scoped analyses with guidance, learn team tools and processes, ask good questions, deliver reliably.

### Key Accomplishments (Past 6 Months)

1. **Weekly Reporting:** Took over the weekly growth metrics report. Hasn't missed a single Monday delivery in 6 months. Made two improvements to the report format based on stakeholder feedback.

2. **Funnel Analysis:** When asked to analyze the signup funnel, discovered a 15% drop-off at the email verification step. Her finding led to a UX change that recovered 8% of the drop-off. She needed significant guidance to scope the analysis (her manager provided the SQL framework and reviewed her code twice), but the insight was genuine.

3. **Team Contribution:** Volunteered to organize the team's knowledge base. Documented 20+ common SQL queries and created a "FAQ for new analysts" page.

### Development Areas

- **Independence:** Still requires detailed guidance for non-routine analyses. When given an ambiguous question ("what's driving the increase in support tickets?"), she struggled for two days before asking for help. Her manager notes she needs to "fail faster" — ask for help sooner rather than spinning.
- **Statistical literacy:** Presented a result as "statistically significant" based on a visual pattern in a chart, without running a significance test. Corrected after code review, but the error reached a stakeholder draft first.
- **Speed vs. thoroughness:** Mika's analyses are very thorough but slow. A routine analysis that should take half a day takes her a full day. She over-checks and over-documents.

### Manager's Summary Note
"Mika is the most reliable person on my team at any level. The weekly report hasn't been late once. She catches things others miss because she's thorough. My question is: is that enough for 'meets expectations' at L2, or does the independence gap and the statistics error pull her below? She's 11 months in — some of this is expected for her tenure. But I've seen faster ramp-ups at the same level."

## Exercise Instructions

### Setup (2 minutes)
- Form groups of 4.
- Each person reads all three profiles silently (5 minutes).
- Each person independently writes their rating for all three profiles on a card or sticky note. **Do not discuss yet.**

### Independent Rating (5 minutes)
Rate each profile:
- **Dana (L3):** Exceeds / Meets / Below
- **Kevin (L4):** Exceeds / Meets / Below
- **Mika (L2):** Exceeds / Meets / Below

For each rating, write a one-sentence justification.

### Group Calibration (8 minutes)
- Reveal ratings simultaneously (hold up cards or read aloud).
- Where the group **agrees**: briefly confirm the reasoning.
- Where the group **disagrees**: discuss. The goal is **consensus**, not compromise. You must agree on a rating for each person.
- Pay attention to what drives disagreements — this is the most valuable part.

### Individual Reflection (2 minutes)
- Write down: Did you change your mind during the discussion? What convinced you?
- Note one thing you would do differently in a real calibration session.

### Debrief Questions
- Which profile generated the most disagreement? Why?
- Did anyone change their mind during the discussion? What convinced you?
- What would you need to see in the manager's log to feel confident in your rating?
- How does "what's expected at this level" change the rating conversation?
