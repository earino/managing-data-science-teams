# SBI Feedback Role-Play — Scenario Cards

Print or display one scenario per pair. Each round, partners swap roles (giver ↔ receiver). Use the **SBI model**: Situation → Behavior → Impact.

---

## Scenario 1: The Silent Stakeholder Meeting

**Context:** You manage Priya, a mid-level data analyst (L3, 2 years on the team).

**What happened:** In yesterday's quarterly product review, Priya had prepared an excellent analysis of feature adoption trends. When the VP of Product asked the room for analytics input, Priya stayed silent. You ended up summarizing her work for her. After the meeting, three people asked *you* follow-up questions that Priya could have answered better.

**Your task:** Give Priya feedback using SBI. The goal is to help her own her expertise in cross-functional settings.

**For the receiver:** You're Priya. You stayed quiet because the VP intimidates you and you weren't sure if your analysis was "good enough" to share in that setting. You're open to feedback but slightly defensive.

---

## Scenario 2: The Heroic Last-Minute Save

**Context:** You manage Marcus, a senior data scientist (L4, 3 years on the team).

**What happened:** Last Friday at 4 PM, Marcus realized the weekly revenue dashboard had been showing incorrect numbers for two days due to a schema change he hadn't caught. He stayed until midnight fixing it and sent a Slack message to stakeholders at 12:30 AM explaining the correction. The fix was excellent. But the error happened because Marcus skipped the data validation checks he's supposed to run after every deployment.

**Your task:** Give Marcus feedback using SBI. You need to acknowledge the heroic fix AND address the skipped validation that caused the problem.

**For the receiver:** You're Marcus. You're proud of fixing the issue quickly and feel the late-night effort should be recognized. You're tired and a bit annoyed that the focus might be on the mistake rather than the save.

---

## Scenario 3: The Overpromiser

**Context:** You manage Aisha, a junior analyst (L2, 8 months on the team).

**What happened:** In a meeting with the marketing team on Monday, Aisha volunteered to build a full marketing attribution model by end of week. This is a 3-week project minimum. When you asked her about it afterward, she said she didn't want to look unhelpful in front of the stakeholders. Now the marketing team expects the model Friday, and you need to reset the timeline.

**Your task:** Give Aisha feedback using SBI. Help her understand the difference between being helpful and overpromising.

**For the receiver:** You're Aisha. You genuinely wanted to help and you thought you could figure it out. You feel embarrassed and worried you'll be seen as unreliable.

## Scenario 4: The Credit Taker

**Context:** You manage Jordan, a senior analyst (L4, 2.5 years on the team).

**What happened:** During last week's all-hands, Jordan presented the churn prediction model results and received praise from the CEO. However, the core modeling work was done by Lena (L3), who Jordan was mentoring. Jordan mentioned "the team's effort" once but used "I" throughout the presentation. Lena came to you afterward and said she felt invisible.

**Your task:** Give Jordan feedback using SBI. This is delicate — Jordan may not have intended to take credit, but the impact on Lena is real.

**For the receiver:** You're Jordan. You thought you adequately acknowledged the team. You feel blindsided by this feedback and a bit defensive — after all, you did mentor Lena through the project.

---

## Scenario 5: The Perfectionist Bottleneck

**Context:** You manage Sam, a staff data scientist (L5, 4 years on the team).

**What happened:** Sam has been reviewing code for two junior team members. Over the past month, Sam's code reviews have averaged 4 days turnaround (team norm is 1 day). The reviews are thorough — dozens of comments, many about style preferences rather than correctness. The two juniors have started avoiding submitting code because the reviews feel punitive. One told you they've been "sitting on PRs for days because I know Sam will tear them apart."

**Your task:** Give Sam feedback using SBI. Acknowledge Sam's standards while addressing the bottleneck and its impact on team morale.

**For the receiver:** You're Sam. You believe in high standards and you're frustrated that the juniors don't write cleaner code. You don't realize the reviews feel punitive.

## Scenario 6: The Positive Feedback Challenge

**Context:** You manage Chen, a data analyst (L3, 18 months on the team).

**What happened:** Chen just completed a stakeholder needs assessment for the product team that was exceptional. She interviewed 8 stakeholders across 3 departments, synthesized conflicting requirements into a clear priorities matrix, and presented it in a way that resolved a 2-month deadlock about what to build next. The Head of Product sent you a message saying "Chen's work just saved us a quarter of wasted effort."

**Your task:** Give Chen specific positive feedback using SBI. The goal is to reinforce exactly what she did well so she (and you) can replicate it.

**For the receiver:** You're Chen. You worked hard on this but you're not sure your manager even noticed. Hearing specific praise would mean a lot to you.

## Scenario 7: The Remote Disconnect

**Context:** You manage Raj, a mid-level data engineer (L3, 1 year on the team). Raj works remotely from a different timezone (3 hours behind the main office).

**What happened:** Over the past two sprints, Raj has been missing the daily standup (which is at 9 AM main office time, 6 AM his time). When he does attend, his camera is off and he gives one-word updates. His actual work output is strong — he shipped two pipeline improvements last sprint. But the product manager complained that "Raj seems checked out" and two teammates said they don't know what he's working on.

**Your task:** Give Raj feedback using SBI. Navigate the tension between his strong output and poor visibility.

**For the receiver:** You're Raj. The standup time is brutal for your timezone. You feel your work speaks for itself and the standup is performative. You're a bit resentful that face-time matters more than output.

## Scenario 8: The Scope Creep Enabler

**Context:** You manage Tanya, a senior analyst (L4, 3 years on the team).

**What happened:** The finance team asked Tanya for a "quick analysis" of Q3 expenses. Tanya agreed and spent 3 weeks building an interactive dashboard with drill-down capabilities, historical trends, forecasting, and department-level breakdowns. The finance team loves it. But Tanya's roadmap work fell behind — she missed the deadline for the retention analysis that was your team's Q4 priority. When you asked about it, she said "the finance team really needed this."

**Your task:** Give Tanya feedback using SBI. Acknowledge the quality of her work while addressing the prioritization failure.

**For the receiver:** You're Tanya. You're proud of the dashboard and you have a great relationship with the finance team now. You feel torn because both projects were important.

## Scenario 9: The AI-Assisted Report

**Context:** You manage Liam, a mid-level data analyst (L3, 1.5 years on the team).

**What happened:** Last week Liam delivered a quarterly market analysis to the VP of Strategy two days ahead of deadline. The VP was impressed — until she noticed that two of the benchmark statistics didn't match her industry knowledge. She checked: the numbers were fabricated by the LLM Liam used to draft the report. Liam had used Claude to generate the first draft and edited it for narrative flow, but didn't verify the external statistics. The VP caught it before it reached the board, but she told you: "I can't trust reports from your team if I have to fact-check every number."

**Your task:** Give Liam feedback using SBI. The AI tool isn't the problem — the missing verification step is. Navigate the tension between encouraging AI-augmented productivity and maintaining analytical rigor.

**For the receiver:** You're Liam. You used AI to work faster and you're proud of the early delivery. You feel the VP is overreacting — "it was just two numbers and they were close." You're worried that admitting the error means you'll be told to stop using AI tools entirely.

## Facilitator Instructions

### Setup (2 minutes)
- Pair students. Assign each pair a scenario (or let them choose).
- Explain the format: 2 rounds, 10 minutes per round (8 minutes practice + 2 minutes debrief), 2 minutes between rounds to swap roles.

### Round 1 (10 minutes)
- Partner A gives feedback (SBI), Partner B receives.
- After 6-7 minutes, stop and debrief within the pair:
  - Receiver: "How did that land? What worked? What felt off?"
  - Giver: "What was hardest? Where did you get stuck?"

### Round 2 (10 minutes)
- Swap roles. Assign a new scenario or continue the same one.
- Same debrief within pairs.

### Full Group Debrief (2 minutes)
- Ask: "What was the hardest part — identifying the behavior, or articulating the impact?"
- Ask: "Did anyone find positive feedback harder than constructive feedback?"
- Key point: "SBI works because it separates what you saw from what you think. That separation is what makes feedback hearable."
