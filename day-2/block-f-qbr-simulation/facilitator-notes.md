## Pre-Class Setup

### Room Arrangement
- **Presentation-ready layout.** If possible, create a clear "front of room" area — a podium, a standing spot, or simply a designated presentation zone. Students should face this area.
- For the simulation itself, students will work in groups of 3–4. Arrange tables so groups can have semi-private conversations during Q&A without disrupting other groups presenting simultaneously.
- If the room allows, have groups spread out so multiple presentations can happen in parallel. If the room is small, do presentations sequentially (adjust timing accordingly).

### Materials Needed
- **Timer visible to students** — This is critical. Each presentation is 5 minutes + 5 minutes Q&A. Use a large projected timer or phone timer. Strict time-keeping models real executive meetings.
- **Exec role cards** — Either printed on card stock (one set per group) or displayed on screen. Each group needs all four roles: CEO, CTO, CFO, VP Product.
- **QBR evaluation criteria** — Printed as a half-sheet for each student, or displayed on screen throughout the simulation. Students use this both for self-assessment and when giving feedback to presenters.
- **QBR outline template** — Students should have this open (digital or printed) as they prepare.
- **Portfolio checklist template** — For the walkthrough at the end.

### What to Have on Screen at 15:30
- Title slide displayed as students enter
- Timer app ready in a separate tab
- Portfolio checklist template ready to project during the walkthrough

## Key Teaching Points

### Must Emphasize
1. **BLUF is the single most important communication skill for analytics leaders.** Keep returning to this during the teaching portion and during debrief. If students walk away with one thing from this block, it should be: lead with the conclusion.
2. **The three executive questions** (Are we on track? What are the risks? What do you need from me?) should become a mental checklist students use for every upward communication.
3. **The simulation is practice, not judgment.** Say this explicitly before the simulation starts. Create psychological safety. Nervous students will perform better if they know this is a learning exercise.
4. **Q&A handling is a skill that requires practice.** Most students have never been grilled by executives. The simulation may be their first experience. Normalize discomfort.
5. **The portfolio is the culmination.** Be specific about what's due, when, and in what format. Ambiguity about assessment creates anxiety that undermines the closing energy.

### Skip If Short on Time
- **Incident Postmortems slides (slides 22–25)** — Important but not essential for the simulation. Can be covered in 60 seconds: "Run blameless postmortems. Focus on systems. Use 5 Whys. Action items need owners and deadlines."
- **Adapting Manager OS by Org Size (slide 29)** — Can be shortened to "Your OS must evolve as you grow. What works at 5 people fails at 50." One minute.
- **Next 90 Days writing exercise (slides 49–50)** — Can be assigned as homework rather than done in class. Save 4 minutes.

### Do Not Skip
- **QBR Simulation Brief and Evaluation Criteria (slides 35–40)** — Students need clear instructions and expectations before the simulation.
- **The simulation itself (slide 41)** — This is the core of the block. Protect at least 35 minutes.
- **Simulation Debrief (slide 42)** — Without debrief, the simulation is just performance, not learning.
- **Portfolio Checklist (slides 43–48)** — Students need clarity on what's due.
- **The Close (slides 53–56)** — End the course well. This is their lasting impression.

## QBR Simulation Logistics

### Forming Groups — Pre-Assign on LMS
- **Post group assignments on the LMS the night before Day 2**, alongside the QBR simulation brief. This saves 2–3 minutes of in-class time and lets you deliberately mix case contexts.
- **Ideal group size: 4 students.** This allows each person to present once and play 3 different exec roles.
- **If 3 per group:** Each person presents once and plays 2 exec roles. Faster rotation.
- **Mix case contexts within groups.** A student presenting a startup roadmap to a panel that includes someone who chose the enterprise context creates richer Q&A. Pre-assignment makes this easy to guarantee.
- **At 15:58, confirm groups.** "Your groups are posted on the LMS. Find your group. First presenter starts in 2 minutes." Skip the counting-off — go straight to first presenter.

### Rotation Mechanics
- Within each group, students decide presentation order. First presenter starts immediately.
- Timer: 5 minutes for presentation. Call time firmly. Then 5 minutes for Q&A. Call time again.
- Between rotations: 1 minute for the next presenter to set up and for panelists to switch roles.
- **If running parallel presentations (recommended for classes >12):** Walk between groups, spend 2–3 minutes observing each. Focus on the Q&A — that's where you can model good executive behavior.

### Timing Math
- 4 students per group = 4 rounds x 11 minutes (5+5+1 transition) = 44 minutes
- 3 students per group = 3 rounds x 11 minutes = 33 minutes
- 5 students per group = 5 rounds x 11 minutes = 55 minutes (tight — consider 4+3 min format)
- **For larger classes:** Run groups in parallel. 20 students = 5 groups of 4, all running simultaneously. You circulate.
- **For very large classes (>25):** Shorten to 4 minutes present + 3 minutes Q&A = 8 minutes per round.

### If Running Sequential Presentations (Small Class <12)
- All students watch each presentation. This takes longer but creates a richer debrief.
- Assign 2–3 students as the exec panel for each presentation. Rotate panel membership.
- Maximum 6 sequential presentations in ~50 minutes (4+3+1 transition each = ~8 min each).

## Playing the Executive Panel

### If the Instructor Joins Panels
- **Be challenging but fair.** Ask the hard questions the role cards suggest, but don't be adversarial.
- **Model good executive behavior:** Be direct, time-conscious, focused on outcomes. Say things like "Can you get to the bottom line?" or "What's the one thing you need from me?"
- **Don't dominate.** Your goal is to model, then step back and let student panelists take over.
- **After each presentation, give brief verbal feedback:** One specific strength ("Your BLUF was crisp — I knew your status in 10 seconds") and one specific improvement ("Your risk section was vague — next time, quantify the impact").

### Coaching Student Panelists
- Before the simulation, remind panelists: "You're playing a character. Stay in role. The CEO cares about business impact, not technical details. The CFO wants numbers."
- Encourage panelists to ask **follow-up questions**, not just the scripted ones. "You said you need 2 more analysts — what happens if I can only give you 1?"
- If a panelist is too soft, gently push: "As the CFO, wouldn't you want to know the cost per analyst?"
- If a panelist is too aggressive, intervene: "Good question — let's let them answer before the next one."

### CRITICAL: Executives Must Interrupt

**Explicit instruction to panelists:** Each executive MUST interrupt the presenter at least once during the 5-minute presentation. This is non-negotiable and must be stated clearly before the simulation begins.

**Why this matters:** In real QBRs, executives interrupt constantly. They don't wait for you to finish slide 12 before asking the question they had on slide 3. Students who only practice presenting without interruption develop a false sense of readiness. The ability to handle interruptions gracefully — answering briefly, then returning to your narrative — is a core executive communication skill.

**Specific interrupt prompts for exec role cards** (share these with panelists):

- **CEO:** "Hold on — before you go further, what's the one thing I need to know?" or "Skip ahead — are we on track or not?"
- **CTO:** "Wait — how does this connect to what engineering is building?" or "What happens if the data pipeline migration slips?"
- **CFO:** "Can you put a dollar figure on that?" or "What's the cost if we don't do this?"
- **VP Product:** "How does this help my team ship faster?" or "My team filed 15 requests last month — when does that get better?"

**Facilitation tip:** Before the first round, say: "Executives, you MUST interrupt the presenter at least once. Don't wait for a pause — just jump in. That's what real executives do. Presenters, your job is to handle it gracefully: answer the question briefly, then get back to your narrative. If you can do that, you're ready for the real thing."

## Evaluation Approach

### Formative, Not Summative
- The QBR simulation is part of the **participation grade (15%)**, not a separate graded component.
- Do not assign individual scores during the simulation. This kills psychological safety.
- **Verbal feedback only** during the debrief — one strength, one improvement per presenter (if time allows for individual feedback) or general patterns across all presentations.

### What to Watch For (Positive Signals)
- Student leads with BLUF — "We're on track for 3 of 4 quarterly goals..."
- Student connects analytics work to business outcomes — "This will reduce churn by an estimated 8%..."
- Student acknowledges risks honestly — "Our biggest risk is data quality in the payments pipeline..."
- Student handles tough questions with composure — "That's a fair question. I don't have the exact number, but here's what I'd estimate..."
- Student says "I don't know" and explains how they'd find out

### What to Watch For (Improvement Areas)
- Student starts with context/background instead of the conclusion
- Student can't articulate business impact — only talks about technical deliverables
- Student gets defensive when challenged — "Well, actually..." or "That's not really the issue..."
- Student tries to answer a question they clearly don't know the answer to (making things up)
- Student runs over time (signals lack of preparation or inability to prioritize)

## Emotional Management

### Creating Psychological Safety
- **Before the simulation, say explicitly:** "This is practice, not judgment. Everyone will be nervous. That's normal and expected. The goal is to learn what executive communication feels like, not to perform perfectly."
- **Normalize failure:** "If you stumble on a question, that's exactly the point. Better to stumble here than in a real QBR with your actual CEO."
- **Frame it as a gift:** "Your panelists are giving you the gift of tough questions. In real life, a CEO who doesn't ask you hard questions isn't engaged — and that's worse."

### For Nervous Students
- If a student is visibly anxious, quietly check in before their turn: "You've been building this material for two days. You know it better than anyone. Just tell them what you know."
- Don't force volunteering for who goes first. Let groups self-organize.
- If a student freezes during their presentation, gently prompt: "Start with your biggest win this quarter. What's the headline?"

### For Overconfident Students
- If a student is dismissive of the exercise, channel that energy: "Great — since you're comfortable, take the CEO role and really push the next presenter. Ask the question they don't want to hear."
- During their presentation, ask genuinely hard follow-ups that force them to acknowledge complexity.

## Portfolio Checklist Walkthrough

### Spend 2 Minutes on This
- Project the portfolio checklist on screen (slides 43–48)
- Walk through each artifact by name: "Team Charter — you started this in Block A. It should be 1–2 pages, final form."
- Be specific about format: "Single PDF or a structured repo with a README that lists all artifacts."
- Be specific about deadline: "One week from today, 23:59 Vienna time. That's [state the specific date]."
- Be specific about submission: "Submit through LMS. One submission per student."
- Answer 1–2 questions if they come up, but don't let this section expand. Keep it crisp.

### Common Questions
- "Can I revise my drafts?" — "Yes, absolutely. The drafts were starting points. Revise, refine, polish."
- "How long should the portfolio be total?" — "There's no page limit. Quality over quantity. A crisp 15-page portfolio beats a rambling 40-page one."
- "Can I use a different format?" — "PDF or repo. If you have a strong reason for something else, email me."

## Closing Energy

### End on a High Note
- The close should feel like a celebration, not a logistics dump. You've just spent two intense days together.
- **Make it personal.** Reference specific moments from the two days: "Remember when [student] nailed that stakeholder map in Block A?" or "The hiring role-play in Block B was some of the best improv I've seen in a classroom."
- **Acknowledge the effort.** "You've built more management artifacts in two days than most new managers create in six months. That's not nothing."
- **Be genuine.** If you're proud of the class, say so. If a particular group or student impressed you, mention it (without singling out anyone negatively).

### The Final 2 Minutes
- Stand at the front. Make eye contact. Slow down.
- Deliver the close (slides 53–56) with warmth and conviction. This is the moment they'll remember.
- After the final slide, stay available for 10–15 minutes for students who want to ask questions, say thank you, or take photos.

## Contingencies

### If Running 10 Minutes Behind
- **Shorten teaching slides** to bare essentials: spend 2 minutes on exec communication (BLUF + 3 questions only), skip postmortems and org size slides entirely. Save 8 minutes.
- **Shorten QBR to 4 min present + 3 min Q&A** per round. Save ~5 minutes across 4 rounds.
- **Keep debrief to 5 minutes** — ask just one question: "What surprised you?"
- Do NOT cut the portfolio checklist or the close.

### If Running 10 Minutes Ahead
- **Expand the debrief** to 15 minutes. Ask for volunteers to share their experience presenting. Facilitate a discussion about what they learned from playing executives.
- **Expand the 90 Days exercise** — have 2–3 students share their commitments with the room.
- **Add a "lessons learned" round** where each student shares one thing they'd tell their past self about management.

### If Technology Fails
- The QBR simulation doesn't require slides — students present from their own notes/templates.
- Write the evaluation criteria and exec role cards on a whiteboard.
- The teaching portion can be delivered from these facilitator notes alone.

### If a Student Refuses to Present
- Don't force it. Offer an alternative: "You can submit a written version of your QBR presentation instead, and play the exec panel role for all rounds."
- Check in privately after class to understand the reason.

### If Presentations Run Long
- Be firm with the timer. "Time. Thank you. Let's move to Q&A." Then "Time on Q&A. Excellent. Next presenter."
- If you're too polite about time, you'll run out of simulation time and some students won't get to present. That's worse than being slightly abrupt.
