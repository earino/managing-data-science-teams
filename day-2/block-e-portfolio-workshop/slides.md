## The Revision Mindset

**Don't polish — strengthen.**

Common mistakes in portfolio revision:
- Fixing typos instead of fixing arguments
- Adding length instead of adding specificity
- Making it look prettier instead of making it more useful

**The test:** Would a VP read this and know what to do next?

<!-- Talk track: Before you start revising, I want to set the right mindset. Revision is not about making things look prettier or adding more words. It's about making your artifacts stronger — more specific, more actionable, more grounded in your case context. The test I want you to apply to every artifact: if a VP at your case context company picked this up, would they know what to do with it? Would they trust it? Would they act on it? If not, that's where your revision energy should go. -->

## Peer Review — What to Look For

**When reviewing your partner's artifact, assess:**

| Dimension | Questions to ask |
|---|---|
| **Completeness** | Any blank sections? Placeholder text? Missing components? |
| **Specificity** | Grounded in case context? Real numbers and names? |
| **Actionability** | Could someone act on this? Or is it just a description? |
| **Reasoning** | Are choices explained? Would a "why?" be answered? |
| **Clarity** | Could someone outside this course understand it? |

<!-- Talk track: When you're reading your partner's artifact, use these five dimensions. You're not looking for typos. You're looking for substance. Is it complete? Is it specific to their case context? Could someone actually use it? Are the choices explained? And could an outsider understand it? Write your observations on the peer feedback form as you read. Be specific — "the rubric section needs behavioral anchors" is useful feedback. "Looks good" is not. -->

## Peer Review — Giving Feedback with SBI

**Apply the SBI model to artifact feedback:**

| SBI Component | Applied to peer review |
|---|---|
| **Situation** | "In the hiring rubric section of your packet..." |
| **Behavior** | "...the scoring criteria use phrases like 'demonstrates competence' without defining what that looks like at each level..." |
| **Impact** | "...which means two interviewers could read the same candidate response and give different scores." |

**Then add a suggestion:** "Consider adding a specific example of what a '3 out of 5' response looks like for each criterion."

<!-- Talk track: You practiced SBI this morning for interpersonal feedback. Now apply it to artifact feedback. Same structure — Situation, Behavior, Impact — but the "behavior" is what you observed in the document and the "impact" is how it affects the artifact's usefulness. Always follow the SBI with a concrete suggestion. Don't just identify the problem — propose a fix. That's what makes feedback actionable. -->

---

## Peer Review — Receiving Feedback

**When receiving feedback:**
- **Listen first.** Don't explain or defend — just absorb.
- **Write it down.** Even if you disagree in the moment.
- **Ask clarifying questions.** "Can you show me specifically where?"
- **Thank them.** They spent time making your work better.

**The hardest skill in management is hearing criticism of your work without taking it personally.** This is practice for that.

<!-- Talk track: Receiving feedback is harder than giving it. Your instinct will be to explain why you made the choices you did. Resist that instinct. Just listen and write it down. You can decide later whether to incorporate it. The ability to hear criticism of your work without taking it personally is one of the most important management skills there is. Your direct reports will need this from you. Your stakeholders will need this from you. Practice it now. -->

## QBR Prep — The Executive Test

**Before you present, ask yourself:**

- Can I state my team's top priority in **one sentence**?
- Can I quantify the **business impact** of my roadmap's top item?
- Do I know the **top risk** and my mitigation plan?
- Am I ready for "**Why not do X instead?**"

**Practice the pitch, then practice the hard questions.**

<!-- Talk track: Run through these four questions. If you can't state your top priority in one sentence, your narrative is too complex. If you can't quantify the business impact, your roadmap isn't tied to outcomes. If you don't know your top risk, you'll be caught off guard. And the "why not" question — that's the one executives love. Why not use a different approach? Why not hire instead of build? Why not wait a quarter? Have your answers ready. Practice with your partner — have them ask you the hardest question they can think of. -->
