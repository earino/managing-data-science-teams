- **Post-lunch energy:** This block is deliberately lighter on new content. Students will be tired after Block D's emotional intensity and the lunch break. The workshop format lets them work at their own pace, which is restorative.
- **Instructor posture:** This block is more coaching than teaching. Circulate during activities. Be available. Answer questions. Look over shoulders and offer targeted suggestions.

### Opening: Acknowledging the Emotional Shift (2 minutes)

Block D is emotionally heavy — PIPs, calibration, giving and receiving difficult feedback. Students need a deliberate reset before shifting to the more constructive, craft-oriented work of portfolio revision.

**Do this in the first 2 minutes, before the title slide:**

1. **Acknowledge the morning:** "This morning was intense. You practiced giving feedback that's uncomfortable, you debated calibration ratings that felt genuinely high-stakes, and some of you sat with difficult feelings about performance management. That's by design — those are the hardest parts of the job."

2. **Run a 90-second reset exercise:** "Before we shift gears, I'd like you to take 90 seconds. Write down one thing from this morning that you want to remember — a lesson, a feeling, an insight. Then set it aside. We're moving from the emotional work of growing people to the craft work of strengthening your artifacts. Different energy, same care."

3. **Name the shift:** "Block E is your workshop. No new frameworks, no role-plays, no discomfort. Just you, your work, and time to make it better. Think of it as the decompression chamber between the deep dive of Block D and the high-stakes performance of Block F."

This transition takes about 2 minutes. It's worth it — students who don't get a clean break from Block D will carry that emotional weight into the workshop and be less productive.

## Activity Facilitation

### Artifact Revision Workshop (30 minutes)

**Your role:** Coach, not lecturer. Circulate actively.

**Common patterns to watch for:**
- **Students polishing instead of strengthening.** If you see someone reformatting instead of adding substance, gently redirect: "The formatting is fine — is there a section where the reasoning could be stronger?"
- **Students working on their best artifact.** Redirect: "Which artifact would you be least confident submitting? Work on that one."
- **Students stuck.** Ask: "What section are you struggling with?" Then point them to the specific rubric criterion. Often the rubric itself tells them what's missing.
- **Students who think they're done.** Challenge them: "Read me your weakest paragraph. Now tell me — would a VP act on that?" Usually surfaces a gap.

**Time management:** Give a "15 minutes left" and "5 minutes left" warning.

### Structured Peer Review (35 minutes including setup)

**Pairing:** Cross-pollinate case contexts. A student working on the small startup context should review work from someone in the enterprise context. This forces both parties to evaluate clarity — if an outsider to your context can understand your artifact, it's well-written.

**The silent reading phase is critical.** Don't let students skip it. Written feedback forces more careful thinking than verbal feedback alone. The written notes also serve as practice for the peer feedback submission (due 48h after Day 2).

**During verbal feedback rounds:**
- Listen for SBI structure. If you hear vague feedback ("this is good"), prompt: "Can you be more specific? What exactly worked well and why?"
- Listen for the suggestion component. SBI alone identifies the issue — the suggestion makes it actionable.
- If a pair finishes early, challenge them: "Now give one piece of positive SBI feedback about the strongest part of their artifact."

**The revision notes phase (6 minutes):** This is the payoff. Students should write 3 specific, concrete changes they'll make before final submission. Not "make it better" but "add behavioral anchors to rubric criteria 2 and 4" or "replace generic stakeholder descriptions with named roles from the MarketBridge case."

### QBR Prep (10 minutes)

**The elevator pitch practice is the most valuable part.** Push students to actually say their pitch out loud, not just think about it. Speaking crystallizes thinking in a way that silent preparation doesn't.

**Common QBR prep pitfalls:**
- Trying to cover everything instead of focusing on 2-3 key points
- Leading with methodology instead of business impact
- Not having an answer for "why should we invest in this?"
- Memorizing a script instead of understanding the argument

### Instructor Q&A (15 minutes)

**Be genuinely available.** This is one of the few unstructured moments in the course. Students who've been holding questions all day will use this time.

**Common questions:**
- "Is my portfolio complete enough?" → Point them to the checklist and rubric.
- "How will the QBR be graded?" → Refer to the participation rubric; it's about clarity, evidence, and handling questions.
- "Can I revise my artifacts after the QBR?" → Yes, the portfolio deadline is one week after Day 2.
- "How much detail is expected in the Manager OS?" → 2-4 pages, specific cadences with frequencies, connected to case context.

## Energy Management

This block is intentionally lower-intensity than Blocks A-D. Students have been absorbing dense content and doing emotionally demanding exercises (SBI role-play, mock calibration). The workshop format is restorative:

- **Working on their own artifacts** gives students a sense of control and progress
- **Peer review** is social but structured — less draining than role-plays
- **QBR prep** builds confidence for Block F
- **Office hours** lets students self-select the help they need

Don't try to add energy artificially. The quiet productivity of a workshop is its own kind of engagement.

---

## Connection to Block F

At the end of this block, students should feel:
1. **More confident** about their portfolio — they've revised their weakest artifact and received peer feedback
2. **Prepared for the QBR** — they've practiced their elevator pitch and anticipated hard questions
3. **Clear on what remains** — they know what needs to happen between now and portfolio submission

**Bridge to Block F:** "You've spent 30 minutes making your work stronger and 30 minutes getting feedback from peers. Now it's time to present that work to executives. Block F is the capstone — everything you've built comes together in a 5-minute QBR presentation."

---

## Connection to Assessment

This block directly supports three grading components:

1. **Peer Feedback Quality (5%)** — The peer review exercise is explicit practice for the peer feedback submission due 48h after Day 2. Students who take this seriously will produce stronger peer feedback.

2. **All portfolio artifacts** — The revision workshop directly improves the artifacts that make up 70% of the grade (Hiring Packet, Roadmap, Manager OS, PGP + Performance Summary).

3. **Participation (15%)** — Active engagement in peer review, asking good questions during Q&A, and genuine effort during revision all count.
